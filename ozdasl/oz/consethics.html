Consumer Ethics

Keywords: survey, contingency table

Description

Introduction

The introduction of the Euro moves the European Union one step closer to becoming a truly “single” market. However almost daily there are issues discussed in the popular press, that brings into question the homogeneity of this market. One of the major motivations of the European Union was to enable expansions of trade amongst member countries. The premise that there are corporate benefits to the European Union has as an implicit assumption that consumers within the member countries behave in the same or similar way. This paper uses Muncy and Vitell’s (1992) Consumer Ethics Scale (CES) to examine whether there are differences between consumers in Northern and Southern EU countries.

Consumer Focus

The CES was developed over several years using a range of items that was refined, reduced and validated to consist of 18 item examining a range of “questionable” consumer behaviours in relation to their dealing with firms (Al-Khatib et al. 1996). These items identified that there were four distance constructs or categories of ethically questionable consumer behaviour, which they labelled as: Actively Benefiting From Illegal Activity; 2) Passively Benefiting; 3) Actively Benefiting From Questionable Action; and 4) No Harm/No Foul.

The CES has been applied in a range of single country and cross-cultural studies. However, none of the comparative works examine attitudes within one trading block or market, such as the EU, NAFTA or CIS. It might be assumed that countries within such trading blocks should possibly be more similar than countries in markets that are not formally "integrated". If any differences were found, the examination of ethical values would be even more pertinent to organisations operating within these regions, as they would be operating within a heterogeneous “unified” market.

North/South Rationale

For the EU to operate as one market it needs to undergo a process of cultural change, such that there is a common identity as well as a common market (Moller 1993, Seed 1993). Such perspective seems to be a utopian ideal, ignoring the realities of “combining” independent states who have different political traditions, histories (even though they are often related), cultures, and in some cases even values (Bohata 1997).

When thinking about differences in today’s Europe, many firstly think of the problems associated with joining countries that have developed under capitalistic markets, i.e. Western Europe, and those that have evolved under a controlled system, i.e. Eastern Europe (Anonymous 1993, Ballew and Schnorbus 1993, Dale 1995). However, if one considers the level of development within countries their may also be a north – south division, which could also influence consumer’s ethical behaviour (Wood 1995, Van Luijk 1997, Rossouw 1994) Priem et al (1998).

While Southern EU countries are not traditional considered to be “developing nations," there are differences across various socio-economic characteristics. That is, Southern countries generally have; lower population growth rates, lower per capita income; fewer years of schooling, higher infant mortality rates and lower proportions of their population live in urban areas. All of these factors, other than lower population growth suggests that Southern EU countries are “less” developed than their Northern neighbors.

The Sample

The focus of the study is to examine the hypothesis that consumers in Southern and Northern regions of the EU have similar views towards what could be considered permissible or unacceptable consumer behaviour. The data used in this study was collected from regionally balanced sample of business students in 8 EU countries, four from Southern EU countries - Italy, Portugal Greece and Spain - and four from Northern EU countries - Scotland, Denmark, The Netherlands and Germany.

The CES instrument was translated and back translated as required to ensure consistency. The CES questionnaire was a self-administrated questionnaire to undergraduate students from each of the countries, there were 962 respondents in total. The sample is regionally balanced with approximately 50% of respondents from each region. The distribution of ages (mean 20.7 years) and gender (52.8% male) is not statistically different within regions or across regions. Overall there was an 8.1% non-response rate and 2% of the questionnaires were excluded from analysis because they were incomplete, resulting in an effective response rate of 89.9%.

Ethical Questions

There are the ethical questions asked in the questionnaire. In each case respondents answered on a 1-5 scale whether they believed the act to be wrong or not wrong.

  1. Changing price tags on merchandise in a retail store
  2. Drinking a can of cola in a supermarket without paying for it
  3. Reporting a lost item as “stolen” to an insurance company in order to collect the money
  4. Giving misleading price information to a clerk for an unpriced item
  5. Returning damage merchandising when the damage is your own fault
  6. Getting to much change and not saying saying anything
  7. Observing someone shoplifting and ignoring it
  8. Lying about a child’s age in order to get a lower price
  9. Not saying anything when the waitress miscalculates the bill in your favour
  10. Breaking a bottle of salad dressing in a supermarket and doing nothing about it
  11. Returning merchandise to a store by claiming that it was a gift when it was not
  12. Taking an ashtray or other “souvenir” from a hotel or restaurant
  13. Using a coupon for merchandise you did not buy
  14. Using an expired coupon for merchandise
  15. Joining a record/book club just to get some free records/books without any intention of buying records/books
  16. Not telling the truth when negotiating the price of an old automobile
  17. Moving into a new residence, finding that the cable TV/telephone is still hooked up, and using it rather than signing up and paying for it
  18. Tasting grapes in a supermarket and not buying any
  19. Using computer software or games that you did not buy
  20. Recording a compact disc/album instead of buying it
  21. Returning an item after finding that the same item is now on sale
  22. Returning merchandise after trying it and not liking it
  23. Spending over an hour trying on different dresses/items of clothing and not buying any
  24. Tapping a movie of the television
  25. Throwing a can out of a car window in order to get rid of it
  26. Dumping garden and household refuse in the forest
  27. Visiting a store to obtain information about a product knowing that you will be buying the product from a cheaper store that does not have informed sales personnel
  28. Purchasing a product that is illegally imported
  29. Telling a sales rep that you will be buying their product when you know that you are buying it elsewhere
  30. Lying about your income on a credit application form
  31. Purchasing a product known to be stolen

Muncy, J.A., and Vitell, S. J. (1992). Consumer Ethics: An Investigation of the Ethical Beliefs of the Final Consumer.  Journal of Business Research 24, 297-311.
